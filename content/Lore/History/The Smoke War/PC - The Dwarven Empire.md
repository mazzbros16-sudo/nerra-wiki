## At A Glance

The Dwarven Empire was once the dominant power of Aedryn, controlling vast territories from its strongholds within the Stoneward Spine. Renowned for its engineering, industry and military strength, the Empire shaped much of the modern world long before the Smoke War.

At its height, the Empire connected distant regions through trade, infrastructure and technological innovation. Its influence could be felt across the entire continent.

The Empire eventually collapsed during the Smoke War following years of rebellion, political instability and military defeat. Though the Empire is gone, its legacy remains visible throughout Aedryn in the form of ancient Holds, forgotten technologies and the societies that rose in its wake.

## Historical Account

_Excerpt from_ Reflections on Empire: A Study of Dwarven Ascendancy _by Historian Corvin Hale, Astral Collegium Press._

---

History has a habit of remembering the end of things more vividly than their beginning.

Mention the Dwarven Empire to most citizens of Aedryn and they will think first of occupation, rebellion and war. They will think of the Smoke War. They will think of the Empire's fall.

Few stop to ask how it rose.

Long before the modern nations of Aedryn took shape, the dwarves carved vast cities into the Stoneward Spine. Where others saw inhospitable mountains, they saw opportunity. Generations of engineers, miners and inventors transformed stone into industry and industry into prosperity.

The Holds became marvels of the age.

Roads stretched further than any that came before them. Trade flourished. New technologies reshaped daily life. Entire regions benefitted from inventions originating deep beneath the mountains.

Success brought confidence.

Confidence became certainty.

And certainty, as history repeatedly demonstrates, can be a dangerous thing.

As the Empire grew, so too did its belief in its own superiority. Many within its leadership came to view dwarven civilisation as the natural future of Aedryn. Other cultures were seen not as equals, but as peoples who would eventually benefit from dwarven guidance, whether they desired it or not.

Expansion followed.

Then conquest.

Territories were annexed. Populations were brought beneath imperial authority. Resistance was met with increasing force. While many regions prospered materially under imperial rule, they did so at the cost of self-determination.

The contradiction sat at the heart of the Empire.

It built.

It innovated.

It unified.

Yet it demanded obedience in return.

For a time, this bargain held.

Then the cracks began to show.

Rebellions became more frequent. Occupied peoples grew restless. Supply routes stretched across a continent increasingly difficult to control. The Night of Red Smoke transformed resentment into resistance, and resistance into open war.

The Smoke War would ultimately destroy the Empire.

What generations had spent centuries building collapsed within a few decades. The Holds fell. Territories declared independence. Former subjects became new powers.

Yet even now, the Empire's shadow lingers.

Its roads are still travelled.

Its machines are still studied.

Its cities are still inhabited.

Many of the nations that define modern Aedryn were shaped by resisting the Empire.

Others were shaped by inheriting it.

Whether remembered as tyrants, visionaries or something in between, the dwarves achieved one thing that history rarely grants any civilisation.

They changed the world.

And the world has never entirely moved on.
