## At A Glance

The Sunders are vast voids that separate the major landmasses of Nerra, known as Shards. Most scholars believe they were created during the aftermath of the Cataclysm, though exactly how and why remains the subject of ongoing debate.

Travel between Shards is possible but dangerous. Most journeys are undertaken aboard specialised airships capable of crossing the immense distances between continents. The Sunders are notorious for violent weather, magical disturbances, and creatures that few are willing to describe in detail.

For most people, the Sunders are little more than a distant feature of the world. For sailors, merchants, explorers and adventurers, they are a constant reminder that Nerra was once something very different from the world that exists today.


## Historical Account

_Excerpt from **Crossing the Edge: A Survey of Modern Shard Travel**, compiled by Cartographer-General Ellias Thorn of the Royal Geographic Society of Velor._

---

There is perhaps no sight in all of Nerra more unsettling than one's first view of a Sunder.

Descriptions rarely prepare the traveller adequately. Most imagine a canyon, a great valley, or some impossibly large ravine. Such comparisons inevitably fall short. A Sunder is not merely a wound in the earth. It is the absence of earth entirely.

The world simply ends.

Across Nerra, the great continental landmasses known as Shards stand separated by vast expanses of empty space. Between them lie the Sunders, colossal voids stretching beyond the horizon, their depths vanishing into darkness so profound that even experienced scholars struggle to explain what lies below.

Most historians agree that the Sunders emerged during the aftermath of the Cataclysm. Prior to that event, ancient records suggest the world existed as a far more unified landmass. Whether the Sunders were created by the Cataclysm itself or merely revealed by it remains a subject of considerable debate.

For ordinary people, such questions are largely academic.

The Sunders are dangerous regardless of their origin.

Travel between Shards remains possible, though never entirely safe. Airships serve as the primary means of crossing these vast divides, their crews often spending years learning the unique hazards associated with Sunder travel. Strong winds, sudden magical disturbances, inexplicable storms and hostile creatures are all common reports from those who make their living navigating the void.

Many captains possess stories they refuse to tell while sober.

Others refuse to speak of certain voyages at all.

It is not uncommon for sailors to describe strange lights drifting beneath their vessels. Some report hearing voices carried on winds where no wind should exist. Entire crews have vanished during crossings under circumstances that remain unexplained. While scholars dismiss many such tales as superstition, the number of similar accounts collected over the centuries has led some to question whether all of them can be ignored.

The creatures that inhabit the Sunders are equally mysterious.

Merchants speak of immense winged predators capable of tearing apart smaller vessels. Explorers tell stories of things moving through the darkness far below, shapes so large that estimating their size becomes impossible. Most such reports remain unverified, though airship captains are generally unwilling to dismiss them outright.

As a result, nearly every nation maintains laws governing Sunder travel.

Some restrict access entirely. Others require special licences, military escorts or approved trade routes. Even in the modern era, crossing between Shards remains a journey most people will never undertake.

For many citizens of Aedryn, the neighbouring Shards might as well be separate worlds.

Perhaps that is why the Sunders inspire such fascination.

They serve as a constant reminder that the world was once something different. Something larger. Something whole.

What was lost during the Cataclysm remains uncertain.

The Sunders ensure that nobody is allowed to forget that something was.

---

_Archivist's Note: While Cartographer-General Thorn's work is widely regarded as one of the most reliable modern sources concerning inter-Shard travel, critics note that many sections rely heavily upon testimony from merchants, sailors and explorers. As with many subjects relating to the Sunders, separating fact from folklore remains a difficult task._
